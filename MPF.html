MPF Summary, Critiques and Suggestions

Current Situation of MPF enrolment (as of March 2023):1

Among the 13% who are not required to join any retirement schemes, most of them are domestic workers or employees aged below 18 or above 65. According C&SD employment statistics, there were 6,600M unpaid family workers in the employed population.

Major Strengths of MPF2

  1. A clearly defined objective and purpose
  2. Wide coverage of Hong Kong's working population
  3. Relatively high level of transparency
  4. Flexible scheme structure
  5. Immediate vesting of MPF mandatory contributions
  6. Segregated custody of MPF assets by qualified custodians
  7. Comprehensive regulation and oversight provided by the MPFA
  8. Defined-contribution scheme avoids the problem of making unsustainable benefit promises, and minimizes distortions in the labour market (MPF study by Alan Siu, 2002)
  9. The range of investment choices is similar to that found in more mature retirement systems, but does come at a cost in terms of relatively higher fund management fees as a percentage of assets under management (survey report by EY, 2012)

Major Issues (all from here, unless specified)

  1. MPF mandatory contributions alone are inadequate to fund a reasonable retirement
    • The current maximum RI of HK$30,000 a month shows that the system is heavily focused towards those on the bottom rungs of the salary ladder
      • Low-paid workers are not able to save enough with only 10% of their monthly salary
      • Well-paid workers' contribution is capped at $1500 which is not enough for proper retirement as well (in terms of replacement rate)
    • Many experts believe that employees need to save at least 20% of their compensation to maintain the same standard of living pre- and post-retirement
    • Singapore CPF requires 37% contribution; Germany 18.6%; Iceland 21.9%
    • Catch-up contributions are available in US and Singapore for old age/families with children
    • Current replacement rate is around 40%, much less than other jurisdictions with top pension systems e.g. Iceland, Denmark, Netherlands which have replacement rate over 75%
    • The anxiety about the inadequacy of retirement income sources and financial circumstances that they expect to deteriorate once retired have prompted many to postpone retirement - extending withdrawal age?
  2. MPF members have limited choice of providers when investing their mandatory contributions ("partial portability")
    • Even though ECA was introduced in 2012, any future employer mandatory contributions will still go to the original MPF scheme selected by their employer
  3. Low participation in voluntary contribution
    • Public perception of high cost and low return in MPF investment - a 1% difference can reduce return by 30%3 (see average FER)
      • Fund return is the most important factor (from 2016-19) affecting members¡¦ choice on MPF investment (other factors include: fees & charges, services, choices of funds, reputation of service providers)
    • 25% voluntary contribution, among that 20% are TVC (1 January 2023 to 31 March 2023)4
    • Low adoption of voluntary contributions highlights the fact that many members either look outside the MPF system for suitable investment options or are not saving enough for retirement
    • Employer voluntary contributions are not widely adopted
  4. Absence of personal financial advice to enhance MPF members¡¦ informed choice5
    • Members have varying degrees of financial literacy. There are prohibitions on trustees / sponsors providing retirement planning advice and recommendations
      • Lack of information (too much uncertainty on MPF investment) reduces employees¡¦ confidence on MPF system especially on protection on housing and medical needs after retirement
      • Members need access to a wide variety of digestible information, along with professional advice, to be able to make appropriate decisions on allocating their MPF investments.
    • More than half of the respondents indicate that they do not work with any financial professionals to help them with household financial decisions and retirement planning6
      • In some reference markets, personal financial advice can add more than HK$ 600,000 to the retirement savings balance over the active working life of a 30-year-old member
  5. Narrow and tightly defined investment restrictions (see asset allocation)
    • Over 20% of asset in pension funds in P7 countries are not equity/bonds/cash7
  6. MPF administration infrastructure is inefficient, inconsistent and creates challenges for members when switching/combine accounts
  7. Insufficient range of products to cater for post-retirement needs especially on retirement income flow
    • The rationale for having a forced saving program is that some people are myopic. This lack of foresight suggests that people would consume too rapidly out of their lump sum withdrawals
      • Low-paid workers would have strong incentive to consume rapidly so as to be eligible for the public pension; or hold a high-risk portfolio, the worst case is losing all the savings and apply for CSSA, OALA, and other means-tested subsidies
    • Since the issuance of the Principles for Developing Retirement Solutions in April 2020, only 3 retirement solutions have been launched by trustees (as of Oct 2022)8
  8. Insufficient independent governance and oversight
    • Currently only 1 INED is required in Hong Kong while in the UK and Australia, majority and at least one-third, respectively, must be INEDs
  9. The MPFSO and associated regulations are outdated
  10. MPF is a gender insensitive system9
    • MPF is the sole compulsory retirement scheme in HK but unpaid homemakers who are predominantly women and women who are not engaged in employment due to disability, chronic illnesses, etc. are excluded from the scheme
    • Many housewives who were previously employees but have already left the labour market or undertaking caring responsibilities that lead to interrupted work histories and employment on a part-time basis in low paid jobs cannot enjoy much retirement protection from MPF and become financially dependent on their family member
    • There are more female CSSA and OALA recipients than males
      • Means-tested subsidies are usually kept at low level to encourage participation in labour market and may not be sufficient to maintain reasonable standard of living
    • Women (men) labour participation rate is 53.82 (65.34) % in 2022
  11. MPF fails to provide enough investment protection for employees10
    • Annualized IRR of 2.7% since establishment is not bad only if this is a steady rate but the returns are highly erratic over the past 2 decades
    • Those who prefer less volatility can choose low-risk, fixed-income-asset-intensive portfolios. Many Hong Kong employees who are risk averse do exactly this, but they discover that over the long term this strategy yields far lower returns than portfolios with a larger concentration in stocks. Chances are that they may not even beat inflation
    • Those who are most risk averse typically are lower-income employees who already suffer from rather small MPF asset holdings, they are also those who can least afford tiny or even negative real returns on their investment
  12. Counterproductive effect of mandatory early retirement saving
    • Young workers have low earnings and are liquidity constrained
    • Rather than worrying about life after retirement, they have more pressing concerns, like saving for their marriage or for the down payment on their home. The 10 percent mandatory contribution rate would mean that they have to borrow money at high interest rates, or to postpone their plans, like getting married or having their first child
    • Younger MPF contributors are less likely to support an increase in contribution rate and the introduction of universal pension using MPF contribution11
      • Average marriage age over 30 in 2020, fertility rate 0.75

Recommendations (mostly from here)

  1. Establish, implement and operate a centralised administration platform (eMPF)
    • Implementation of eMPF should follow a big bang, rather than staged implementation
    • eMPF should be able to handle straight-through processing (STP) to take over transaction processing responsibilities from trustees.
      • Digitalization could be a problem for disadvantaged groups with limited access to internet connectivity and digital literacy, potentially worsening financial inclusion12
    • All members should have a unique account identification number (ID) to be used consistently within the MPF system, regardless of which trustee(s) / sponsor(s) looks after their MPF account(s)
  2. Allowing "full portability" to lower management fees
  3. Increase the level of independent governance and oversight of MPF schemes
    • Increase the minimum requirements for independent non-executive directors (INEDs) on boards of trustees.
    • Create a separate, independent governance and oversight board at the MPF scheme level.
  4. Continue to update the MPFSO as required, giving the MPFA more discretion and flexibility to react to changing environments.
  5. Increase the level of mandatory and voluntary contributions into the MPF system
    • Consider means to increase mandatory/voluntary contributions and contribution caps to enable faster growth of assets under management (AUM) and achieve scale benefits.
    • The role of trust in the Hong Kong government to deal with MPF issues was the strongest individual determinant of support to both MPF reform options
      • How to enhance MPF contributors trust in government given the fact that the level of general trustfulness in the local government among Hong Kong citizens is currently at such a low level?
    • MPF system is still small in comparison to jurisdictions with more mature system (in terms of pension asset as % GDP)13
    • If the ceiling on contribution is not abolished, the ceiling should be adjusted annually and automatically according to a mechanism (e.g. consumer price index)
    • Introduce employers' compulsory MPF contribution for employees aged 65 or above
    • Encourage employees' contribution for spouses or other family members who are not in employment
  6. Introduce alternative asset classes, such as private equity, venture capital, infrastructure, and commodities
    • Establish a more structured periodic framework to review investment restrictions to open up MPF assets to other viable investment options on a timelier basis.
    • Offer benefits of diversification and additional return drivers.14
  7. Introduction of a wider range and selection of post-retirement products or a standard decumulation pathway
    • Facilitate the establishment of Constituent Funds specifically for the decumulation phase, only accessible for +65 years.
    • Facilitate a higher proportion of retirees remaining in the MPF System and benefit from low pricing, ease of product switching, and decumulation product innovation.
    • Provide a degree of protection by offering a default solution deemed suitable for the typical consumer, such as annuity or insurance.
    • More products targeting households of the 3rd income quartile who cannot afford to save much through TVC and other private pension schemes.
    • Example in Chile: accumulated benefits have to be used to purchase an indexed annuity or to be drawn down periodically subject to a maximal rate of withdrawal.
      • Some concerned that the flexibility of the scheme might be low and that the principal may not be readily available to meet urgent needs.
      • The policy question is whether to require all retirees (or those with accrued benefits less than a minimum?) to buy an annuity and to coerce insurance companies to use a single life table for all members of a retiring cohort. Government intervention is needed to overcome the problem of adverse selection, thus resulting in a more efficient pooling of mortality risks.
  8. Introduce more gender equality elements, such as grant per child for women, economic compensation in case of divorce, and survivorship pensions for men.
  9. A "universal" basic pension for all citizens will be an effective means to alleviate the poverty of old age women without any stigmatizing effect of means-tested public assistance schemes.
  10. The government can offer a "risk of return insurance" that guarantees a minimum rate of return (say at 2 percent after inflation) for all years for all cohorts. It taxes "excess returns" whenever a year brings returns higher than the guaranteed real rate of return and uses the proceeds to subsidize "short returns" whenever a year brings returns that are lower than the guaranteed real rate after inflation.
    • Since the market typically affects members in very similar ways, with members' returns rising and falling together, in a bad year, it is not possible that there are enough high return members to subsidize the returns of low or negative return members. The "cross subsidization" that occurs is inter-temporal. The government has to collect sufficient taxes during the good years.
  11. More inflation-protected fixed income products
    • May not be feasible since HK as a small open economy has not much control over monetary policy.
  12. Dis-aggregate the tax incentives for MPF voluntary contributions (TVC) and QDAP
    • Provide a separate tranche for TVC and another for DA premiums.
    • Raise the deductible limit for each tranche to HK$100,000, which is in line with the other allowances, such as child, education, and home loan interest.
  13. Let young workers, e.g., aged less than 30, opt-out of the MPF system to spare the savings for more pressing needs15
    • Given the low level of support to any of the proposed MPF reform options, future studies should be undertaken to gain a better understanding of the short- and long-term consumption and savings preferences particularly among younger MPF contributors in Hong Kong.
  14. The government can commit to phase out the old-age CSSA programs after say, 20 years, and start making top-up contributions for low-income workers now
    • The top-up contributions are not spending but are savings for future obligations under the current CSSA system. The difficulties are how to define the cutoff point for making top-up contributions and to decide on the level of the top-up.
  15. Review the need for an affordable, high quality, mass-market financial advisory infrastructure that is attractive for providers to offer and which members will use to maximize their long-term retirement savings
    • A survey done by Gain Miles indicates that two-thirds of interviewed employees wish to have professional advisory services/risk assessment on MPF investment while only 1 quarter wish to have more investment education sessions.16
    • Those who do reach out for help from financial professionals most prefer a financial planner/advisor at a bank, followed by an insurance agent/broker.
  16. More structured/standardized guidance for ESG investments
    • Low-carbon investment mainly obtains the carbon emission data publicly released by each company, which is more objective compared to ESG scoring systems by different investment companies.
  17. Introduce taxes on luxury goods, undesirable behavior e.g., pollution, smoking to generate revenue for retirement protection measures.17

Additional notes on other possible pension plans

Critiques on pay-as-you-go system
  • A comprehensive pension system has three important functions: saving, redistribution, and insurance. The PAYG system found in many countries often combines the three functions into a single program and is often organized as a Ponzi scheme:

    (...) Everyone who reaches retirement age is given benefit privileges that far exceed anything he has paid in. It stems from the fact that the national product is growing at compound interest and can be expected to do so for as far ahead as the eye cannot see. Always there are more youths than old folks in a growing population.

  • Even though the benefit formulas are seemingly progressive, the amount of redistribution is often limited because the rich enter the labor force later and tend to live longer.
  • Slower growth and financial market volatility (and increasing unemployment in some countries) are challenges for maintaining financial sustainability of pension systems.
  • Reduces saving incentives and brings adverse impact on future economic development (see pension debt in other economies). 19
  • The appropriate generosity of the PAYG system needs to reflect a country's political culture and, in particular, the acceptability of different levels of taxation and compulsory contribution. 20
Critiques on means-tested welfare program
  • Create a major disincentive for low- and middle-income workers to save on top of their mandatory contributions for their retirement. It also discourages older workers to continue working after attaining the retirement age.
  • It can be inequitable. Suppose two workers, A and B, have the same lifetime earnings. Worker A does not save for retirement and consumes all his disposable income, while worker B saves by investing in his children. Worker B's children would wind up supporting the retirement of both A and B.

Areas to enhance employers' satisfaction on MPF 18

  • Dedicated relationship manager
  • Shorter processing time
  • e-channel for data submission
  • e-channel for employee enrolment

3 Major Concerns of Employers on eMPF platform 21

  • adequate customer support when encountering problems
  • sufficient network security measures for privacy protection
  • unclear roles and responsibilities between the eMPF Platform company and MPF providers would lead to administrative inefficiencies

Notes from MPFA Annual Report 2021-22

  • Complaints from MPF trustees and service providers concerning scheme administration account for 14% of total complaint issues.
  • Over 80% of complaints are on issues like default contribution and non-enrolment, half are from employers in construction and catering industry.

Chairman annual letter to investors about retirement investment (Larry Fink, BlackRock CEO)

  • Even in wealthier countries, many people lack the ability to save; and if they do save, they often use those savings for an emergency, rather than investing for retirement. In some countries people are actually over saving but under-investing. If they are keeping their money in the bank rather than investing in the market, they won't generate the returns necessary to retire with dignity.
    • 67% Hong Kong people have saving habit, among that, only 27% save for retirement. Over 70% put their savings as demand/time deposits 22
    • Having insufficient income is the major hindrance of not saving
  • Long-term investing requires trust in the financial system and a fundamental belief that tomorrow will be better than today.

Public opinions on retirement protection 23

  • Some views reflected that some elderly persons, despite having accumulated $100 000 to over $1 million in savings, were still worried because they did not have a stable monthly income. (...) it is worried that limited savings may not be enough to cope with the expenses for retirement life as long as tens of years and other various unforeseeable expenses particularly medical expenses.
    • It was not common for elderly persons to buy health insurance for themselves. Their medical expenses were mainly paid by their savings or their children. It was clear that those who were covered by medical insurance believed they had a more stable retirement life.
  • There were views suggesting that the eligibility for additional allowance should be based on other criteria, such as seven years of residence in Hong Kong, having worked in Hong Kong for a certain period of time, having paid tax, or other non-asset related criteria.
  • Most proposals disregard self-owned properties from the means test, but some proposals set limits for self-owned properties. Individual proposals include additional assessment criteria such as rental expenses, family and health status, etc.
  • Ageing parents and delayed childbearing cause financial pressure on retirees as they are still the breadwinners of their families (incomes decrease while expenses remain more or less the same)

** a comprehensive survey report on Hong Kong people's retirement planning and investment: SOA Research Report 2018 (Section: Retirement from the Consumer Perspective)

Suggestions from Survey on Retirement Readiness of Middle-income Group

  • Raising retirement age of civil servants
  • Introducing online video medical consultation and medication management service to reduce travel and waiting time as well as allowing mobility-impaired patients to receive appropriate treatments
  • Simplifying application procedures and longer interest-free period for property maintenance loans targeting elderly in the 3rd income quartile
