Financial Preparedness and Saving Adequacy - Overview

Top concerns on retirement

  • Inflation
    • Retirement Survey & Insights Report 2022 done by Goldman Sachs indicates inflation to be the biggest concern for retirees in the US. Among current retirees, 19% have difficulty making ends meet in a typical month and 24% report that their lifestyle in retirement has fallen short of pre-retirement expectations.1
  • Healthcare expense
    • Surveys show that health problem is one of the top worries over retirement. Older individuals are particularly stressed about future healthcare expense as they may not have enough time to close the saving gaps.2, 3
      • Figure 1: Percentage of respondents worry about health problems
  • Running out of money
    • Not saving enough to sustain the retirement life ranked the 2nd in Fidelity Global Sentiment Survey 2022. A research studying the household financial situation in Singapore states that respondents expecting over 30% chance of running out of money in the future is not quite possible given the average household net worth and monthly consumption spending, reflecting the excess anxiety on retirement.
      Figure 2: Percentage of respondents worry about using up retirement savings early
    • Workers across all regions and age groups indicated that they find the retirement planning process very complicated which is reflected from the significant difference in people's perceived readiness versus their objective readiness, over-confidence was observed.4
      Figure 3: Percentage of unprepared individuals, perceived vs. objective estimation
  • Reduction in retirement benefits
    • A number of countries, such as Germany, Japan, and Korea, have done some reform to cut the payout from their public defined benefit plan or tax breaks for pensioners to sustain the system under a rapidly aging society.
    • Social security payment (pillar 0) is not often indexed to inflation making this more worrying to retirees in recent years.

Psychological Dispositions

Individual saving decisions are affected by one's cognitive capabilities, the ability to plan over time, and the perseverance required to carry out those long-range plans.

  • Future time perspective
  • Retirement goal clarity
  • Level of financial knowledge
The average level of future orientation was not only stronger in the USA than in The Netherlands, but it was also more strongly related to Americans' perceptions of savings adequacy than among the Dutch. Thinking about the future and saving for retirement are two separate issues in The Netherlands, whereas these issues are clearly linked in the USA.

Social Forces

Individual saving decisions are shaped by the social context in which they are made.

  • Spouse or partners
  • Friends and co-workers
  • Early parental socialisation
The effect of social support is more indirect. Spouses influence planning practices by extending one's time perspective further into the future. The support of friends and colleagues is likely to impact on the clarity of an individual's retirement goals.

Institutional Forces

Individual saving decisions are shaped by the quality and design of pension institutions.

  • Trust in government
  • Trust in banks / fund managers / insurance companies
  • Trust in employer pension
Structural differences in employer and state pensions in the Netherlands and United States lead to very different saving pressures on the individual worker. Private pension may force workers to think about (if not actually save for) their future retirement. A lack of trust in the government may provide strong incentives to save by motivating individuals to fend for themselves in the retirement financing arena while the positive relationship found between trust in one's employer pension and perceived savings adequacy was much stronger among the Dutch, which is unexpected (when trust in a governmental pension is high and retirement resources needs are expected to be relatively low).

**some simple demographic information (e.g. age, marital status, education level) is also studied.

Perceived barriers to retirement readiness

  • Not earning enough
  • Paying down debt
  • Haven't thought about retirement yet
  • Lack of financal knowledge
  • Don't know where to start with retirement preparation

Factors associated with objective retirement readiness

The following are factors that were found to be associated with individuals' financial preparedness for retirement. Causation is not implied in some findings.

Time inconsistency: poor understanding of the time dimension of a person's life

Young people believe that they will be able and willing to work longer to compensate for inadequate pension savings but when they get older, they are very likely to regret not saving enough and cannot retire earlier.

  • More than 40% of working respondents believe their savings are behind schedule. The younger respondents are more likely to report their savings are ahead of schedule.
  • Nearly three quarters of working respondents plan for too little retirement income.
  • One-fourth of non-retired adults have no retirement savings.

Behavioural biases (e.g. loss aversion, illusion of control) due to use of language and presentation make annuities and other retirement products less desirable.

Generating retirement income and understanding how long the saving will last are the top challenges for both working and retired respondents. Many people therefore prefer consistent, stable or guranteered life income while the popularity of purchasing annuity is low in the same survey.

Figure 5: Preference on ways of generating income during retirement

More than 50% of younger workers with a lower Retirement Readiness Score were invested too conservatively. Low proportion of equity investment was observed across all generation in Canada. Many respondents expect to reduce expense once retired in the hope that the savings would last longer but the reports suggest that this is not a good strategy as there are too many uncertainties in the years of retirement.

Choice architecture is widely used in many jurisdictions (e.g. autoenrollment/opt-out pension plan, orange envelop in Sweden, DIS of MPF) to improve the efficiency of consumer decision regarding retirement investment.8 The author also suggested "phased annuitization", a gradual purchase of annuities over time, to increase the sense of control over one's personal assets.

Age/Generation

Report from IFEC indicated that more retirees reported relying on HKSAR's old age benefits, while younger generations are increasingly less likely to count on children in their old age. The way people prepare for retirement has changed.

Figure 6: Planned or actual source of retirement income

Older individuals are more prepared than younger workers in most surveyed economies (except HK and Germany) but whether this is due to generational or age difference is unclear.9

Early retirement age

Due to higher life expectancy, more savings have to be accumulated to maintain one's pre-retirement living standard during a longer retirement. Model estimates that countries with higher retirement age allow individuals to accumulate more wealth through retirement plan such that more of the salary can be used on pre-retirement daily expenses.
Labour force participation (LFP) rate of population aged 65 or above has been rising in many developed countries including Singapore and The US. People might be involuntarily remaining in the labour force due to inadequate household finances for retirement.10

Figure 7: Labour force participation rate in Singapore

Majority of the respondents in UK, US, Australia and Canada have indicated the preference to delay retirement. Canada shows the same preference across all income basket, reflecting the desire to work longer is not only out of financial needs. Another survey in the US presents the possible reasons behind.11

Figure 8: Major reasons people work in retirement

However, in reality nearly 50% of the retirees retired because of reasons that are out of control, only 7% retired because of having sufficient fund. One-third retired due to health-related issues making part-time work, which is the most popular option of generating retirement income, not a possible solution.

Educational attainment

Research found correlation between old age labour force participation and education level. Low-educated workers are also more likely to stay in the workforce longer. As shown in the chart below, the drop in LFP of low-educated group as age increases is less than the more educated groups.

Figure 9: Labour force participation rate (a) and Self-reported retirement (b) by educational attainment in Singapore

Low-educated group is also more likely to report that they were in a worse-than-expected financial situation around age 45.

Figure 10: Percentage of household in Singapore (by education) in worse-than-expected financial situation

Relationship between educational attainment (as well as income and socio-economic status) 12 and financial preparedness for retirement seems to be more indirect, through the level of financial knowledge.13

Household net worth/income

Low household net worth is also associated with old age labour force participation and worse financial situation. The study found out that the lower educated and low net worth were more likely to have a worse than expected situation due to a negative health shock to themselves (or spouse) which resulted in large, unexpected health expenses or affected their ability to work, which implies the importance of adequate access to healthcare.

Figure 11: Labour force participation rate (a) and Self-reported retirement (b) by household net worth in Singapore
Figure 12: Percentage of household in Singapore (by household net worth) in worse-than-expected financial situation

Gap was found between NRRI14 and self-reported statistics. Households across the income distribution underestimate their level of risk, especially in higher-income households. People were too optimistic on their asset value due to strong housing and stock market performance when the housing loan is not fully repaid. 15

Figure 13: Percentage of "at risk" individuals in different income groups from NRRI (2019) and self-reported data

CRR paper also states that homeowners, when their home represents a much higher portion of their total net worth, are more likely to be "too worried" because they do not plan to tap their home equity to support general consumption in retirement. The use of reverse mortgages for funding retirement is also not widely anticipated (11%) among homeowners, downsizing is much more popular (48%).16 The feeling of losing the property might be a reason.

Individual level of debt

Repaying debt is often a more immediated financial needs and the debt level sometimes associates with financial literacy as well.

Conflicting saving needs

Saving for retirement is complicated by managing other more immediate financial needs (e.g.loans, finacial support for family, time out of workforce) especially among younger generations. Both surveys by IFEC and PORI agree with this. 17

  • Close to half of respondents report at least once in their career the need to stop saving for retirement due to financial hardship.
  • One-third (33 percent) of workers and one-quarter (26 percent) of retirees, especially those who consider debt to be a major problem, agree that retirement savings is not a priority relative to the current needs of their family.18

Employment and participation in retirement plan

Among those who are saving or intend to save, two in five (40 percent) workers globally cite employment reasons (i.e. employer started paying into a retirement plan, automatically enrolled into their employer's plan, employer offered a matching contribution to their plans) being a factor that prompted them to save.19

Individual's savings and investments amount was found to be positively related to the participation in retirement plan.20 Countries that don't require high personal savings rate usually have either high tax rate or retirement plan mandatory contribution rate to support a high pension payout in retirement.21 The absence of auto-enrolment to workplace pension in Germany may contribute to the low score of readiness across all ages and income groups.

Young workers in HK score significantly higher than older groups due to higher contribution from retirement savings. Rising awareness from the introduction of MPF is likely to be one of the reasons.

Continue decline in interest rate & slower global economic growth

Over a decade of low-interest rate and low portfolio return (slower growth due to pandemic and rising interest rate) makes it hard for individuals to accumulate enough savings for retirement through low-risk investments and may even suffer from losses. UBS's report also illustrates how a stable return from pension plan helps people to save for retirement without sacrificing current needs.

Unemployment

People around the globe have reported the negative effect of COVID-19 on their income and savings. In Hong Kong, mature working adult was affected most due to income losses and increase expenses on family care according to IFEC.

High level of public debt and deficits

If future retirees cannot rely on social security to the same extent as previous generations due to reduction in public retirement benefit, this means that personal engagement, action, and a sense of ownership and accountability is of paramount importance. This is a critical in countries providing state pension especially those giving unsustainably generous payout e.g. Germany and Switzerland.

Numeracy 22

Many studies found out that, it is numeracy rather than financial literacy that is important to net household total wealth, financial stress, and retirement income expectations. Despite the strong correlation between numeracy and cognitive ability, a lot of heterogeneity in numeracy cannot be explained even when examining a narrow age group in the population and when looking at those with similar levels of cognitive ability.

Other suggestions

  • Encourage high-skilled older workers to delay retirement to maintain high level of productivity in aging society.
    • Delayed retirement maintains the size of the labour force, but it is mainly driven by low-skilled workers.
    • Introducing alternative work arrangement, such as flexible working hours, remote working, disability accommodations, could be more effective in raising the employment of high-skilled older workers.
  • Holding the budget constant, enhancing social insurance against unexpected, catastrophic events, such as major illness or bankruptcy, could be more effective in improving social welfare than one-off cash transfer programs.
    • Help guarantee the minimum quality of life even under the rare event of catastrophic income shocks, while the latter policies increase disposable income might have only moderate impacts from a life cycle perspective.
  • Negative or positive phrasing can impact responses due to acquiescence bias.
    • Surveys in which a high share of households report concern for their retirement income use negative or neutral words like "concerned" or "on-track" while surveys that report a low share use positive words like "confident/confidence".
  • Incentivize employers, schools, and trade groups to provide programs to help individuals update and learn new skills that will prepare them for a changing workforce and longer time spent working.
    • Being laid off or not able to get a new job is also a reason why people retire earlier than planned.

UBS International Pension Gap Index

The report evaluates retirement readiness through studying the gap between living cost and income in retirement of an average Jane, who is a 50-year-old (in 2020) single woman earning median wage and hasn't yet planned for retirement, in the major city of 24 jurisdictions.

The above graph shows the percentage of disposable income Jane needs to save starting from age 50 in addition to pension plan contribution in order to maintain her current basic urban lifestyle in retirement in different jurisdiction. The calculation is based on the assumption that the savings and the pension capital is invested.

  • 0-20%: start retirement saving at 50 may work
  • 20-50%: manageable if one starts young (saving rate may not be possible in cities with high living cost)
  • >50%: save as you go
*the index is time-specific as economic conditions changes
